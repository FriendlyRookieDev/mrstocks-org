What is the difference between CFD trading and stock trading?

Among the most well-known securities are shares. They are a securitized share of a shareholder in the equity of a company. In Germany, the German Stock Corporation Act (AktG) is responsible for the rights and obligations of the shareholder.

The trader is not a shareholder in the company when he buys the CFDs. He merely becomes the holder of a claim. The starting point is a derivative, which is derived from the underlying value based on the price of the CFDs. The underlying is called the underline. It can be shares, but also other securities, such as commodities and indices. Compared to the purchase of stocks and shares, the CFD investor participates in the price performance of the financial instruments of the providers. For this reason, CFD trading can be classified as financial contracts for difference.

In most cases, CFD trading takes place over the counter. You trade the CFDs as Over the Counter – short OTC. This means that they are not settled directly on the stock exchange through the CFD broker, but directly through the trading partners such as Société Générale. Société Générale does the hedging and secures the entirety of the position overhang, which is called exposure.

Development and history of CFDs

The major bank UBS developed the first Contracts for Difference in the 1980s. They wanted to use them to avoid the British stamp duty. At that time, every transaction with shares on the London Stock Exchange had to be taxed additionally with 0.5%. Today we know that contracts for difference are an invention from investment banking. Thus, there is a legal way to avoid stock exchange fees or taxes in over-the-counter trading between a trader and a CFD broker.

CFDs are financial derivatives that are traded over-the-counter as OTC trades. Behind the three letters stands “over the counter”. The trading volume grew with the beginning of the 1980s and the CFD market became larger and larger.

In Germany, we owe it to a decree of the Federal Financial Supervisory Authority – BaFin for short – that private investors are allowed to do CFD trading without margin requirements since 2017. This is an important safety criterion, so that traders do not lose more than the capital that they have invested and that is in the account with the provider. Another restriction was decreed by the European Securities and Markets Authority in August 2018 for the entire EU territory. The restrictions amounted to the elimination of the margin requirement for CFD trading as well as the minimum margin requirements for the operation of maximum trading.

How do CFDs work?

Whether with or without a demo account: Most should have gotten at the closer impression of what is actually meant by CFD trading. Now we want to take a look at how CFDs work. Thereby we dedicate ourselves to the most important concepts in the CFD trade:

  • the trading sizes,
  • the spreads,
  • the holding period,
  • profits and losses.
  • Spread and Commission

You can find CFD prices in two quotations. In the technical language we talk about the bidirectional prices. Depending on the broker, the functions differ. But the price distinction always remains the same: there is a selling price and a buying price. By the selling price or the bid price, we mean the price at which you open a short CFD. The ask price or the purchase price opens a long CFD. In most cases, the broker will have the selling price of the stock CFDs slightly lower than the market price. The buy prices are often slightly higher. These two quotations result in the difference, which is called the spread.

In most cases, the spread covers the costs incurred by opening a CFD position. For this reason, the selling and buying prices must be aligned with the fees incurred to complete the CFD trade. It doesn’t matter if you use leverage or a demo account. These rules for trading are the same everywhere and apply to your account as well.

The trading size

Stock CFDs are traded in so-called lots. This means standardized contracts. The underlying should correspond to the size in which this CFD is traded on the market. We will illustrate this with a short example:

On the commodity exchanges, for example, the commodity silver is traded at 5,000 troy ounces per lot. In turn, you can assume that a silver CFD is traded for 5,000 troy ounces. A contact is usually equivalent to a share on the exchanges. If we now again assume 500 CFDs contacts, you would buy 500 shares, for example. At this point it should become clear that CFD trading is much more similar to traditional trading than we think it is. This is what differentiates stock CFDs from other options, spreads and pips when trading.

How to trade on rising and falling prices at the CFD broker?

CFD trading offers every trader the opportunity to speculate on rising and falling prices and profit from them. In technical language, the rising prices are called long trading positions and the falling prices are called short trading positions. With a CFD broker, you can go long or short at any point in time. This brings into play a great advantage in trading directly with the particular underlying asset. Let’s look at the two options a little closer at the CFD broker:

Go long – you buy a CFD when you expect the price to rise and go long. Later, you want to sell the CFD at a higher price.
Going short – When a trader expects a falling price at a CFD broker, he goes short and sells a CFD first. Later, he wants to buy back the CFD at a lower price.

In CFD trading, there are no limited terms. This brings the advantage that you are not subject to any time value loss. Exceptions are the so-called futures CFDs, in them is a defined contract end with a specific maturity date. With one of the best CFD brokers you have the possibility to hold your CFD positions for days, weeks or months. For a longer period, pay attention to the conditions that a CFD broker brings for holding overnight positions. Basically, when trading CFD stocks, day trading, i.e. short-term trading, can be distinguished from medium-term trading.
